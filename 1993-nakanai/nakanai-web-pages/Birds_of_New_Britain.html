Expedition Bird List

The Birds of New Britain

After Coates, B. J.
"The Birds of Papua New Guinea"
(1985 & 1990)

®=Restricted-range species *=Species endemic to New Britain
Ý=Threatened species (Ý)=Near-threatened species
DD=Data-Deficient species

For more details see Collar et al, 1994.

Those species that were recorded by the Nakanai '93 expedition are underlined and a brief summary of our observations is given.

Dwarf cassowary Casuarius bennetti (Ý)
Little grebe Tachybaptus ruficollis
  • Recorded Lake Hargy only.
Streaked shearwater Calonectris leucomelas
Wedge-tailed shearwater Puffinus pacificus
  • One individual seen off the north coast, 15/8.
Heinroth's shearwater Puffinus heinrothi ®Ý
  • Flock of about 20 birds, presumed to be this species, off the north coast, 15/8.
Brown booby Sula leucogaster
Little pied cormorant Phalacrocorax melanoleucos
Greater frigatebird Fregata minor
Lesser frigatebird Fregata ariel
  • Frequent off the north coast west of Bialla - see Hornbill Point
Great egret Egretta alba
  • Reasonably common though well dispersed along certain stretches of the coast
Eastern reef egret Egretta sacra
  • Very common along stretches of the coast, on the shore and on the reefs. Sometimes congregating in quite large groups (up to about 30 birds). Both colour phases though the white form commoner
Mangrove heron Butorides striatus
Nankeen night heron Nycticorax caledonicus
  • Two records. Hornbill Point and Mount Talawe.
Black bittern Dupeter flavicollis
Water whistling duck Dendrocygna arcuata
  • Recorded Lake Hargy only
Spotted whistling duck Dendrocygna guttata
Australian black duck Anas superciliosa
Osprey Pandion haliaetus
  • Recorded Lake Hargy and Hornbill Point
Crested hawk Aviceda subcristata
  • Seen at Lake Hargy and Mount Talawe. Probably a reasonably common raptor on New Britain, but elusive
New Britain buzzard Henicopernis infuscata ®*(Ý)
  • Recorded on several occasions at Wild Dog and twice during our trek towards the Nakanais, once at 1100m. One also seen over secondary lowland forest close to Bialla
Brahimny Kite Haliastur indus
  • A common and conspicuous raptor on New Britain, recorded everywhere we went. Usually single individuals, occasionally pairs
White-breasted sea eagle Haliaeetus leucogaster
  • Apparently regular but infrequent along the coast of New Britain. We recorded it at Bialla, Wallindi, and an immature at Wild Dog (over primary forest at an altitude over 1000m)
Meyer's goshawk Accipiter meyerianus
New Britain sparrowhawk Accipiter brachyurus ®*Ý
Slaty-backed goshawk Accipiter luteoschistaceus ®(Ý)
  • Small hawks, which must have been this and/or the preceding species, were recorded quite frequently but only one was ever seen well enough to be positively identified: the lack of a neck collar made it this species. It was seen flying just above the groud through a cocoa plantation near Rabaul. Other small hawks were most frequently seen in open habitat such as gardens; they probably frequented forest too but would be much harder to spot in this habitat
Variable goshawk Accipiter novaehollandiae
  • A reasonably common forest raptor, recorded at a number of dispersed sites on the island
New Britain Grey-headed goshawk Accipiter princeps ®*(Ý)
  • A pair seen displaying over Leli village on the trek to the Nakanais. Display dove-like but slower and more graceful. Both individuals circled then one would climb steeply with exagerated flapping and then stall and glide down with half-closed wings. Also, a single bird recorded at Wild Dog
Oriental Hobby Falco severus
  • Only recorded in north, at Wild Dog and from road out of Rabaul, but considered to be at high density at Wild Dog where they were usually observed in pairs. One pair could always be found siting in the same tree near camp
Peregrine falcon Falco peregrinus
Melanesian scrub fowl Megapodius eremita ®
  • Known to be extremely common in places on New Britain, notably Pokili, we only ever recorded single individuals near Lake Hargy and at Mt Talawe
King quail Coturnix chimensis
Red-backed button quail Turnix maculosa
Banded landrail Rallus philippensis
Pink-legged rail Rallus insignis ®*
White-browed crake Poliolimnas cinereus
  • Recorded Lake Hargy only
Bush hen Amauroornis olivaceous
Purple swamphen Porphyrio porphyrio
Comb-crested jacana Irediparra gallinacea
  • Recorded Lake Hargy only
Black-winged stilt Himantopus himantopus
Lesser golden plover Pluvialis dominica
Mongolian sand plover Charadrius mongolus
Large sand plover Charadrius leschenaultii
Whimbrel Numenius phaeopus
  • Recorded Hornbill Point only
Common sandpiper Tringa hypoleucos
  • Individuals or pairs seen at various points along the coast including Rabaul, Kimbe and Hornbill Point. Also inland at Mount Talawe
Grey-tailed tattler Tringa brevipes
  • Recorded Hornbill Point only
Red-necked phalarope Phalaropus lobatus
Common tern Sterna hirundo
  • Very occasionally sighted at sea and off Hornbill Point
Black-naped tern Sterna sumatrana
  • Recorded once: Hornbill Point
Bridled tern Sterna anaethetus
  • Flocks of up to about 20 birds fairly regular at sea and off Hornbill Point
Sooty tern Sterna fuscata
Little tern Sterna albifrons
Crested tern Sterna bergii
  • A very common tern all along the coast; occurs in large numbers
Common noddy Anous stolidus
White-capped noddy Anous minutus
  • Another very comon tern all along the coast in larg numbers
Superb fruit dove Ptilinopus superbus
Red-knobbed fruit dove Ptilinopus insolitus®
  • Apparently the commonest fruit dove on New Britain. Recorded from many primary forest sites
Yellow-bibbed fruit dove Ptilinopus solomonensis ®
White-bibbed fruit dove Ptilinopus rivoli
  • Reasonably common in primary forest, though difficult to observe
Torres Strait pigeon Ducula spilorrhoa
  • Quite a common, though apparently rather localised, pigeon on New Britain. Nest found on Hornbill Point
Black imperial pigeon Ducula melanochroa ®(Ý)
  • Quite common in undisturbed forest. Usually in flocks flying overhead or roosting in the canopy of a tall tree. Recorded at Lake Hargy, Wild Dog and Mount Talawe
Grey imperial pigeon Ducula pistrinaria ®
  • Recorded in small flocks at Hornbill Point and offshore from Walindi
Red-knobbed imperial pigeon Ducula rubricera®
  • A good population at Mount Talawe; possibly also at Wild Dog and Lake Hargy. A pair seen during trek to Nakanais
Finsch's imperial pigeon Ducula finschii ®(Ý)
  • Reasonable population at Wild Dog; possibly also at Mount Talawe and Lake Hargy
D'Albertis' mountain pigeon Gymnophaps albertisii
  • Quite a common bird at higher altitudes on New Britain. Usually in flocks
Yellow-legged pigeon Collumba pallidiceps ®Ý
Brown cuckoo dove Macropygia amboinensis
  • A very common bird in all forest habitat throughout New Britain. Usually in pairs. Frequently singing/calling
Bar-tailed cuckoo dove Macropygia nigrirostris
Pied cuckoo dove Reinwardtoena browni
  • One record: Wild Dog (1000m)
Stephan's emerald dove Chalcophaps stephani
  • Recorded in Glosta area only (Mount Talawe and on coastal road between there and Kilenge
Beccari's ground dove Gallicolumba beccarii
  • Recorded Wild Dog only. Prominant white eye-ring
White-breasted ground dove Gallicolumba jobiensis
New Britain Bronzewing Henicophaps foersteri ®(Ý)
Nicobar pigeon Caloenas nicobarica (Ý)
Rainbow lorikeet Trichoglossus haematodus
  • A very common bird on New Britain. Flocks recorded in most areas we visited
Eastern black-capped lory Lorius hypoinochrous ®
  • Another common bird but not at such high density as Rainbow lorikeet. Usually seen in pairs. Nest hole found at Mount Talawe
Red-chinned lorikeet Charmosyna rubrigularis®
  • Occasional flocks at several dispersed sites from lowland gardens to montane rainforest. Often in company of red-flanked lorikeets
Red-flanked lorikeet Charmosyna placentis
  • As for Red-chinned lorikeet
Buff-faced pygmy parrot Micropsitta pusio
  • Recorded Wild Dog only
Red-breasted pygmy parrot Micropsitta bruijnii
Blue-eyed cockatoo Cacatua ophthalmica®*
  • A common bird throughout New Britain in all habitats. In groups of 1 to 8. A nest hole found at Mount Talawe was in the side of a dead tree about 20m above the ground
Eclectus parrot Eclectus roratus
  • Another common bird throughout New Britain in most habitats, although apparently much scarcer at higher altitudes. Usually in pairs
Song parrot Geoffroyus heteroclitus®
  • Found to be locally common, mainly in undisturbed forest. Nest holes found at Wild Dog
Bismark's hanging parrot Loriculus tener ®(Ý)
Oriental cuckoo Cuculus saturatus
Brush cuckoo Cacomantis variolosus
  • Seen once (in a garden in Kimbe) but heard regularly at a number of sites
Shining bronze cuckoo Chrysococcyx lucidus
  • Recorded Wild Dog only
Koel Eudynamis scolopacea
Channel-billed cuckoo Scythrops novaehollandiae
  • Recorded Mount Talawe only
Violaceous coucal Centropus violaceus®
  • First observed at Mount Talawe. With hindsight, probably heard at Wild Dog also
White-necked coucal Centropus ateralbus®
  • Recorded at many sites but nowhere abundant. From lowland plantations to montane rainforest. Singly or in small groups. Regularly heard calling in the forest
Golden owl Tyto aurantia ®*Ý
Bismark bobook owl Ninox solomonsis ®
New Britain bobook owl Ninox odiosa®*
  • The same owl call was heard regularly at a number of different sites including regularly at Wild Dog (1000m). It was presumed to be this species
Large-tailed nightjar Caprimulgus maerurus
Glossy swiftlet Collocalia esculenta
  • Locally abundant, particularly at high altitude
Uniform swiftlet Collocalia vanikorensis
  • Reasonably common throughout the island
White-rumped swiftlet Collocalia spodiopygia
  • The common swiftlet around the lowland plantations, though it was also recorded at higher altitudes
Moustached tree-swift Hemiprocne mystacea
  • Common throughout the island in all habitats
River kingfisher Alceco atthis
  • Recorded Hornbill Point and Mount Talawe only
Bismarcks kingfisher Ceyx websteri®
  • Recorded Lake Hargy and along the river at Mount Talawe. It was observed fishing at Lake Hargy: it would dive into the water from a perch (a bush or fallen branch) and then return to the same branch, occasionally with a small fish in its beak which would be quickly swallowed
Little kingfisher Ceyx pusilla
Dwarf kingfisher Ceyx lepidus
  • Seen once at dusk near camp at Mount Talawe, otherwise only ever recorded when netted (Lake Hargy, Wild Dog and Mount Talawe)
White-mantled kingfisher Halycon albonotata®*
  • Two pairs: Wild Dog (1000m) and Mount Talawe
Sacred kingfisher Halycon sancta
  • Common throughout the island in all habitats and at all altitudes
Collared kingfisher Halycon chloris
  • One record: Hornbill Point
White-headed kingfisher Halycon saurophaga
  • One record: on an island off Walindi
Black-headed paradise kingfisher Tanysiptera nigriceps ®
  • One record: Wild Dog
Blue-tailed bee-eater Merops philippinus
Rainbow bee-eater Merops ornatus
  • Flocks encountered quite regularly throughout the island in most habitats
Dollarbird Eurystomus orientalis
  • Lone pairs of these birds encountered at 3 or 4 different sites
Blyth's Hornbill Aceros plicatus
  • A common, though usually dispersed, bird on New Britain in most habitats but particularly primary rainforest. An exceptional roost of over 300 birds on Hornbill Point each night. Otherwise, generally singly, in pairs or in small groups
Blue breasted pitta Pitta erythrogaster
Pacific swallow Hirundo tahitica
Tree martin Hirundo nigricans
Black-faced cuckoo-shrike Coracina novaehollandiae
White-bellied cuckoo-shrike Coracina papuensis
  • Regular in pairs or small groups (1-4) in most habitats throughout the island
Yellow-eyed cuckoo-shrike Coracina lineata
  • Recorded Wild Dog only
Cicadabird Coracina tenuirostris
  • Reasonably common in the Bainings (Wild Dog) area at all altitudes. Not recorded elsewhere
Varied triller Halage leucomela
  • Quite a common forest bird, recorded in most areas. Usually in small groups
Pied chat Saxicola caprata
Island thrush Turdus poliocephalus
Northern Melanesian ground thrush Zoothera talasea ®(Ý)
Rufous-faced thicket warbler Megalurulus rubiginosa®*
  • One record: Wild Dog (1000m)
Grosvernor's thicket warbler Cichlornis grosvenori ®*DD
Australian reed warbler Acrocephalus australis
Island leaf warbler Phylloscopus trivirgatus
Tawny grassbird Megalurus timoriensis
Golden-headed cisticola Cisticola exilis
  • Several recorded in scrub beside road near Glosta. Also possibly at Wild Dog
Bismark rufous fantail Rhipidura dahli®
  • Occasional records in primary forest, particularly at higher altitudes, though probably a reasonably common bird in the forest interior
Northern fantail Rhipidura rufiventris
  • A reasonably frequent forest bird (more so than Bismark rufous fantail). Recorded Lake Hargy, Wild Dog and Mount Talawe
Willie wagtail Rhipidura leucophrys
  • Common in towns and gardens; also at Lake Hargy and Hornbill Point. Usually in pairs
Spangled drongo Dicrurus bracteatus
  • A common bird throughout the island in all habitats. Usually in small groups; very vocal
Bismark pied monarch Monarcha verticalis®
  • Recorded fairly regularly in all forest habitat
Satin flycatcher Myiagra cyanoleuca
Shining flycatcher Myiagra alecto
  • Individuals (both males and females) recorded at a number of sites including gardens and rainforest
Lesser shining flycatcher Myiagra hebetior ®
River flycatcher Monachella muelleriana
Common golden whistler Pachycephala pectoralis
  • Recorded infrequently, in primary forest, but probably reasonably common
Mangrove golden whistler Pachycephala melanura
Ashy honeyeater Myzomela cineracea®
  • A very common bird in the forest. Usually singly or in small groups
Red-tinted honeyeater Myzomela cruentata
  • Occasional records in primary forest. Individual or pairs
New Britain red-headed honeyeater Myzomela erythromelas ®*
  • Quite frequent in forest habitats. Individually or pairs. Often singing
New Britain friarbird Philemon cockerelli ®
  • Very common and very vocal in all habitats. Groups of 1 to 5
New Britain honeyeater Vosea whitemanensis ®*
Black sunbird Nectarinia aspasia
  • Reasonably common in all habitats
Yellow-bellied sunbird Nectarinia jugularis
  • Occasionally encountered in gardens and other disturbed habitats
Bismark flowerpecker Dicaeum eximium ®
  • Recorded in small numbers at all altitudes, mainly in forest habitat
Black-headed white-eye Zosterops hypoxantha ®
  • Small flocks common in undisturbed habitat
Hooded mannikin Lonchura spectabilis
Buff-bellied mannikin Lonchura melaena ®
  • One record: a flock of about 40 birds foraging on short grass lawns in coastal gardens in Kimbe
Blue-faced parrot-finch Erythrura trichroa
Singing starling Aplonis cantoroides
  • A bird of towns, villages and other disturbed habitats, though not common anywhere. Occasional records in more pristine habitats
Metallic starling Aplonis metallica
  • Flocks common in all habitats throughout New Britain. Trees decorated with hundreds of nests were occasionally discovered but there was never any sign of breeding taking place whilst we were there
Yellow-faced myna Mino dumontii
  • A very common bird in all habitats throughout the island. Usually in pairs. Frequently observed sitting at the top of a tree duetting with a partner
White-backed wood-swallow Artanous insignis ®
  • Small groups occasionally recorded in forest and gardens at all altitudes
Torresian Crow Corvus orru
  • A common bird throughout the island but never in large numbers
