Suggestions for Further Study

Nakanais

The central Nakanai plateau is still a largely unexplored area. Who knows what biological wonders await discovery there? The area still needs to be surveyed to confirm that it holds a unique community of plants and animals. Such a survey should give weight to the proposal that the mountains be designated a national park.

In addition to just compiling an inventory, it would be very useful to carry out population censuses for any species found there which are range-restricted or considered threatened. Also, to collect information on the habits and requirements of these species. Such data would be invaluable in assessing the action necessary for the conservation of these species.

Lake Hargy

Our time at the lake was limited and we were unable to explore much beyond our campsite. Perhaps the best way to further explore the lake would be by boat, although there is the obvious danger of the crocodiles to be considered.

The main aim of any further study should be to assess the lakes tourism potential: to discover a suitable site for a simple lodge; to assess the ease and safety of navigation on the lake; to locate suitable landing stages around the lake from where it would be possible to trek into the forest; to compile a more complete species list (birds, mammals, butterflies, etc). Obviously it would be necessary to consult the West New Britain Tourist Bureau before any such exploration went ahead.

Before any tourism venture is put into practice at Lake Hargy, it is essential that the crocodile population be studied to assess

  • (a) whether they are likely to prove dangerous to tourists, and just as importantly
  • (b) whether tourism on the lake is likely to disturb the crocodiles
If either of these seem likely the venture may have to be dropped. If tourism does go ahead it will be necessary to continually monitor the crocodile population and other wildlife so that appropriate action can be taken should they suffer any ill effects from the disturbance.

Wild Dog

If mining does go ahead it will be necessary to draw up a work plan that ensures minimal disturbance to the surrounding forest. Indeed, the mining company should ensure that any forest not destroyed is given full protection within a reserve to prevent any further disturbance, and that when operations have ceased any cleared land is replanted with local tree species.

The Bainings mountains rise to 2400 m and, according to Beehler (1993) they have not been adequately surveyed. A series of biological studies carried out at different altitudes throughout the range would provide valuable information on the geographical and altitudinal range of the species that live here.

The local villagers report that dwarf cassowaries live in the forests around Wild Dog. This is a near-threatened species and very little is known about its habits. It is, however, known to be a vital component of the forest ecosystem: seeds of certain forest plants have to pass through the gut of a cassowary before they will germinate. A study of this species is necessary to assess what steps need to be taken to conserve it and the plants that depend upon it. Research is equally needed into other near-threatened species in the area.

Max Henderson of the Pacific Heritage Foundation is the person to contact regarding conservation research in the Bainings. He is keen to encourage international interest in the area and his Riet Project. He can be contacted at Ulatawa Estates Pty. Ltd., P.O. Box 1342, Rabaul, PNG. Tel: (675) 92 1294; fax: (675) 92 1197.

Glosta

A lot of primary forest does still remain in the Glosta area, but it appears that most of it will soon be destroyed by reckless logging companies. The most biologically diverse areas of forest must urgently be identified and given protection. There is little doubt that these forests are species-rich, but to confirm this researchers must get away from the extensive garden areas and into virgin rainforest. The local landowners need to be educated in the scientific value of their forest and about the disasterous consequences of signing their land over to the foreign logging companies.

If tourism is to be the alternative to logging, trails need to be mapped through the areas of primary forest and special sites identified in order to provide good walking and nature-watching opportunities for tourists. A fuller species inventory should be compiled and local villagers trained as guides so that they can show visitors whatever it is that they have come to see.

The Hornbills need further research. How extensive an area do the birds roosting on the point fly in from? How big is the population at Glosta? Where do they breed; dispersed throughout the forest or are there specific areas where many birds nest? What is there most important food source - again, is it dispersed or localised? Such information is vital if the impressive spectacle on the point is to be preserved in the face of current logging practices.

Marsha Berman is the Director of Programmes for the West New Britain Provincial Tourist Bureau. She wrote to us after our return to the UK, her letter included the following plea:

"West New Britain would greatly welcome the presence of study groups, now and in the future; they can expect every possible assistance from the authorities in facilitating their stay in our province. In return, all we would like to ask is for them to spend some time and attention on some specific problems or an area of study, collecting data which would support our environmental protection and conservation efforts... We will do everything in our power to assist you, as the work you do will, in the long run, assist us..."

One area in particular which may be interesting for future field study trips is the Whiteman Range which has been identified as a World Heritage Site by the World Bank/Tropical Forestry Action Plan Field Study, but which has not yet been researched and consequently is now also under threat of being logged away."

General

Strategies need to be drawn up for the conservation of biologically important areas and threatened species. The first step is to identify where those areas are and what the requirements of those species are.

The broad areas of biological importance on New Britain have been identified - see the next section. However, little is known about the most significant sites within these broad areas; for example, specific breeding areas for important species. The exploration of an entire mountain range, in order to assess which sites have the greatest biological importance, would be a mammoth task, it is simply unfeasible. Instead, either the entire area needs to be given protection, or, 'umbrella species' need to be chosen: an umbrella species should be one that is usually only to be found in species-rich communities; if actions are taken to preserve this species, then a diverse range of other species will also be conserved.

Birds listed as threatened may make good umbrella species. Research into, and actions to conserve, threatened species, should lead to the conservation of biologically important areas. At present, so many of New Britain's birds are so little known that any assessment of their status (threatened or otherwise) is little more than an educated guess. Even less is known about the survival requirements of those birds. Research is desperately needed into these issues, if the unique species on New Britain are not to be lost due to habitat destruction.

It is not just the threatened species that are poorly understood. The ecology of so many of the birds that live on New Britain still remains a mystery. Despite this, birds are the most well researched taxonomic group on the island. Other groups are much less well understood and yet in equal need of conservation efforts.

Beehler has written a paper on research priorities in Papua New Guinea (Beehler 1991), in which he stresses

  • The necessity of consultation with traditional land-owners and local naturalists, and
  • The need to take into account seasonal and supra-annual variations in climate and forest cycles
Also, the importance of long-term human interactions with the forest, which have considerable impact on forest ecology, must not be forgotten.

A great deal of research is needed to assess the ecology and status of the fauna and flora of New Britain, but, with the current rate of deforestation on the island, we cannot afford to wait for this research to be completed before action is taken to conserve that wildlife.
