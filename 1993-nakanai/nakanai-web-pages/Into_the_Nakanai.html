Into the Nakanais

A short flight transported us from the dust and grime of Port Moresby to the lush green surrounds of Hoskins airport on New Britain. We were met by a truck from Hargy Oil Palms Pty Ltd at whose guest-house we stayed during our time in Bialla. John Wjynand, manager of the plantations, was a generous host, going out of his way to ensure that our preparations went ahead smoothly and efficiently. It wasn't long before our route to the plateau had been charted, six weeks of supplies had been purchased and we were ready to start on the last leg of our journey.

A truck took as down a very rough track to Kamatami village but could go no further; from then on we were on foot. Walking was incredibly hard, trekking through the forest on poor muddy tracks; climbing many steep ridges and crossing numerous rivers. However, the hardship was compensated for by the thrill of venturing further and further into a little-known and incredibly beautiful land with views that included a not-too-distant smoking volcano cloaked in rainforest.

Walking with us were two employees of Hargy Oil Palms who came from Kamatami village and thus knew the area and people. In addition, during each stage of the journey we had up to 30 "carriers" from the previous village hauling our supplies to the next village. We spent nights in the villages of Kamatami, Lele and Muela where we received great hospitality from the inquisitive villagers. After a total of three days hike we arrived in Mile and the Nakanai plateau was in sight, sheathed in mist just a few kilometres away.

Our euphoria was soon tempered by discussions with the locals. The villagers of Mile and Mukul (the two closest villages to the uninhabited plateau) regarded the hills as a "bad-land" full of evil spirits not to be visited. Without their co-operation, we had no hope of getting into the range as, without guides, entering the sharp, jagged hills would have been too dangerous. The villagers pointed out the all too obvious steepness of the ridge facing us, the many land-slide sites and the lack of any paths. We had three such ridges to cross before reaching the true plateau and not one of the villagers had ever been beyond the top of the first one. It became clear that the Nakanai expedition was not going to happen and the whole team felt emotionally drained and depressed.

On reflection however the trek had not been worthless. We have at least proven the inaccessibility of the mountains and have also confirmed that they are covered in pristine rainforest, rarely if ever visited by humans; not even the local villagers who fear to venture beyond the first ridge.

Access to the central Nakanais is only really possible by helicopter, certainly if the aim is to spend any length of time there. Probably a clearing would first have to be made by somebody dropped in on the end of a rope; this, we were told, is how a team of geologists managed to land a helicopter at the top of the first ridge. A caving expedition also entered the Nakanais by helicopter (Gill 1988). Water supplies could pose a problem for any expedition attempting this as the rock is porous there are apparently no surface streams or pools (we had planned to collect rain-water in large tarpaulins).

The inaccessability of the Nakanais, whilst being the ruin of our expedition, is actually a positive feature. Loggers have not touched the mountains' forests and whilst exploitation remains possible in the lowlands they are unlikely to do so. Only when heli-logging becomes economically feasible will these mountains come under threat. Thus there is still time to safeguard this, one of the last truly untouched wildernesses on earth. Furthermore, protection for the area is unlikely to be against the wishes of the locals who themselves lay no claim to the land.

Finally, our trek taught us much that was to prove useful in the weeks that followed. For example we were crazy to expect to maintain high spirits on a diet of rice and tinned meat day in day out. We sold off these provisions in the villages on our return to Bialla where it was decided we should consider alternative projects.

Five unhappy and exhausted people arrived back in Bialla to receive two pieces of very good news. In our absence, faxes had arrived from the Pacific Heritage Foundation and the West New Britain Provincial Government (WNBPG), both requesting our help. With these, the shape of the rest of the expedition was decided in a day: A week by Lake Hargy at the base of the Nakanai range and the rest of our time split between a site in the Bainings and a site near Mount Talawe in the Cape Glosta area - the latter two in response to the faxes we had received.
