Discussion and Inferences

It is clear from the results of the study that there are differences between the bird communities of the two sites. The question is, are these differences the same as those predicted in the introduction? Are the differences what we would expect from patterns of human disturbance?

A Reduction in Total Number of Species

More species were recorded at Wild Dog than at Talawe: 55 at Wild Dog; 43 at Talawe. The outcome of the sign test leads us to conclude that there is a real difference between the number of species at Wild Dog and at Talawe. Talawe undoubtedly has fewer species of birds than Wild Dog. This may well be due to the habitat changes that have occurred at Talawe.

A Decrease in the Variety of Niches Occupied

The outcome of the G-test does not allow us to conclude that the birds of any particular diet-type are significantly more susceptable to disturbance. If Talawe originally had a bird community identical to Wild Dog, which has since been reduced due to human disturbance, the diet type of a species did not necessarily have any effect on its chances of survival or extinction following disturbance. However, the non- significance of the statistics does not prevent us from making reasonable biological inferences about the species composition (according to diet type) of the bird community at Talawe compared with that at Wild Dog.

Looking at the diets of the species recorded, we see that, with the exception of the lichens, all the diet categories are represented by at least one species at each site. Only one of the feeding types (lichens) are actually missing from Talawe. This is not surprising: the categories are all very broad; each contains many potential niches; virtually an entire food type would have to be destroyed to loose all species from that catagory. The lack of any lichen feeders is not so surprising considering that only one lichen-feeding species is present at Wild Dog. This is presumably quite a specialised niche which may have been destroyed by the habitat changes at Talawe.

Within each diet category, more species occur at Wild Dog than at Talawe, with the exception of "vegetarians" and "omnivores: true generalists". This suggests that at Talawe certain specialist niches may have been lost, and possibly the resources available have been partitioned into broader niches such that fewer species can co-exist within each category. This could be due to few species being highly specialised for the new habitat.

Due to the assumed reduction in plant diversity at Talawe one would expect there to be fewer vegetarian species there. Instead there are the same number. Most of the vegetarians on the list are actually frugivores. It could be that such birds do well at Talawe due to the cultivation of fruiting trees in the gardens, thereby retaining, or creating, a diversity of frugivore niches. Levey ('88) claims that frugivorous birds are much more abundant in tree-fall gaps, perhaps this applies for larger cleared areas too.

Two or three trees were found in full flower in some of the taller forest at Talawe. These trees were the only site where the red-flanked and red-chinned lorikeets were found (both primarily nectar feeders). None of the trees at Wild Dog were similarly in flower; if some had been during our stay, perhaps these two species would have been recorded there too.

The fact that the only group in which more species were recorded at Talawe than at Wild Dog was the "true generalists" is illuminating. We could infer that the lack of many specialists at Talawe leaves resources available for the accommodation of some new generalist species. However, this explanation seems less likely if we look at the actual species involved. Stephan's emerald dove and Becarri's ground dove occupy very similar ground-foraging niches; one occurs at Wild Dog, the other at Talawe. This leaves crested hawk and Melanesian scrubfowl. Both of these are actually primary forest birds, which have apparently survived the habitat changes at Talawe, probably in part due to their opportunist lifestyles. It is possible that these two species were also at Wild Dog but went unrecorded, having very unobtrusive natures.

The Abundance of a Few Common Species, Which are Likely to be Opportunists/Generalists

If we compare the abundance and scarcity of the species, ever bearing in mind the error inherent in the density measures, our predictions are again borne out. It is quite clear that the commonest species at Talawe are more abundant than those at Wild Dog. (Note: the high abundance of these common species does not necessarily reflect a high quality habitat. A high concentration of individuals quite frequently occurs in what is actually poor quality habitat for that species. For example, the high quality forest may be occupied by territorial pairs, forcing large numbers of non-breeders into the disturbed habitat. See Van Horne '83.)

The rainbow lorikeets appear to be exceptionally abundant at Talawe. However, although they were very numerous, the estimate has probably been inflated by the large numbers concentrated at a few flowering trees, as mentioned earlier.

We would predict that the successful species at Talawe are so because they are adaptable generalists, and this too appears to be true. Seven of the nine abundant species at Talawe are omnivores; four of these, including the three commonest species, are true generalists. The two exceptions are both vegetarians; reasons have already been given to explain why vegetarians might be successful at Talawe.

This uniformity of diets amongst the commonest species at Talawe compares with a far wider range of diets among the commonest of the Wild Dog birds. Only four out of the ten most frequently observed birds are omnivores, of which only one is a "true generalist", and two are "nectar + insects" feeders (a specialist category amongst the omnivores). Of the remaining common species, four are vegetarians and two carnivorous.

A further point worth mentioning is that three of the abundant species at Talawe (rainbow lorikeet, metallic starling and Torresian crow) are widespread opportunist species that can also be found in Port Morseby (the capital of PNG) on mainland New Guinea. No such species are amongst the abundant birds of Wild Dog.

Furthermore, all of the abundant species at Talawe are species that were seen regularly throughout our stay on New Britain, and they were all recorded in the spot counts at Wild Dog. In contrast, the Wild Dog common species included some species that we rarely encountered elsewhere: black-headed white-eye, song parrot, D'Albertis' mountain pigeon and glossy swiftlet; the latter three, plus moustached tree swift, were not recorded in the spot counts at Talawe and D'Albertis' mountain pigeon was not recorded at Talawe at all.

A Reduction in the Number of Scarce Species.

In addition to the common species at each site, for which density estimates were made, a further 23 species were recorded at Wild Dog compared with 16 at Talawe. 22 additional species were recorded at Wild Dog but did not feature in the spot counts; 18 additional species were recorded at Talawe. Wild Dog obviously supports a much greater variety of scarce species than Talawe, particularly those that are regular (recorded in spot counts) but not common. There is not a huge difference between the two sites in number of very scarce species (those not recorded in the counts at all). Perhaps many of these very scarce species were only recorded at Talawe because they inhabit the pockets of primary forest and nearby extensive undisturbed forest.

A Decrease in Diversity

The rank-abundance graphs clearly show that the Talawe bird community is considerably less equitable than the bird community at Wild Dog. The Talawe graph is steeper than the Wild Dog graph showing that a smaller number of species make up a larger proportion of the total number of individuals at Talawe. This, coupled with the lower species richness at Talawe, gives the site a much lower diversity than Wild Dog, as shown by Simpson's diversity index. The diversity index for Talawe (9.19) is less than two-thirds of the index for Wild Dog (15.79).
