Getting Started

Oxford University: reputedly the pinnacle of British academia (sorry Cambridge!) Yet is it really such an exhilarating place to be? Why do so many students, having climbed the arduous ladder to reach this, "the top", then spend half their time longing to get away again? Certainly I was no exception. I wanted to go somewhere tropical, an island with silver sands and palm trees, exotic birds and coral reefs.

So, I sat down with a friend and an atlas and we decided that Papua New Guinea seemed like a worthy destination. What to do there? Deciding this involved a lot of discussion and research and many letters were written before we eventually whittled it down to kingfishers on the discovery that this was the kingfisher family's centre of origin. Luckily a book had just been published on the world's kingfishers (Fry et al 1992) and from this we learnt of the Moustached kingfisher: a large bird confined to Bougainville Island. First described in 1904, a dozen specimens of this kingfisher were collected in the late 1930's but there have been no more records since then (it was also found on Guadalcanal Island, one specimen in 1941 and two in 1953, but it is now thought to be extinct there). Our expedition was soon settled as "The Search for the Lost Kingfisher of Bougainville".

Now one point in Oxford's favour is that it does provide the opportunities. At least, it is true to say that if I wasn't at Oxford I would never have got this expedition off the ground. However, all that 'Oxford' actually provides is the inspiration, and the assurance that such way-out schemes really are possible - there is a long list of "Oxford expeditions" that have been successful in the past and whose reports are lodged in one of the libraries. When it comes down to it though, all the hard work is down to you.

This involved a huge amount of research and organisation, and the biggest task of all: raising sponsorship. We produced a prospectus, acquired approval from the Oxford University Expeditions Council, and then applied to hundreds of grant giving bodies and private companies both at home and in Papua New Guinea. We wrote to scientists with experience of the area we were heading for, we became affiliated with the University of Papua New Guinea and of course we applied for visas from the P.N.G. High Commission. First-aid seminars were attended and equipment was gathered. We even had expedition T-shirts printed.

There was so much to do and nothing was easy. Getting money from private industry at a time when the whole country was gripped in recession was like getting blood out of a stone. We had rather more success with grant giving bodies and were particularly pleased to win a prize in the Birdlife International Conservation Expedition Competition. We also applied to a number of companies for help in kind and received some generous responses. Trimble Navigation granted us use of a Global Positioning System (GPS) whilst KJP Photographics loaned us a complete camera outfit. The British Library Of Wildlife Sounds (BLOWS) provided us with a high quality tape recorder plus microphone in return for recordings of some of the birds we encountered and thus a new dimension was added to our project.

It should be mentioned here that our plans to search for the Moustached kingfisher were dropped just as we were about to complete our prospectus outlining the proposed project. This sudden turn-around was instigated by a press cutting which gave an update on the civil war that was raging on Bougainville and which had somehow totally escaped our attention! The replacement aim, decided upon after discussion with our patron Bruce Beehler, was to study the avifuana of the Nakanai Mountains on New Britain Island and it is outlined in the following section. Just before this upset my partner in this venture pulled out and I had the tricky job of finding a replacement. Nick was the chosen one and shortly afterwards we recruited a further two members; whilst we were in P.N.G. a technician from the University made our team five.

Our final headache were our visas. Despite having applied for them months in advance and many frantic faxes/phonecalls, they didn't arrive on time and we were forced to leave without them! Fortunately we had a week in New Zealand before heading on to Papua (this was the cheapest flight deal we could find) and we got everything sorted there. Then it was time for the real adventures to begin. Most of the rest of this report is an account of what we achieved whilst we were out there.

After our return home we were very quickly caught up in the dizziness of approaching finals. But now with our exams out of the way we turn our thoughts back to Nakanai '93 to write this report. Our experiences are now memories and, although we all claim to be better people thanks to the trip, the real importance of our work lies within this document. We want others to learn from our experiences and we hope that this report will be an inspiration to those in whose hands the destiny of New Britain's forests lie. We can vouch for the beauty, interest and value of those forests as they stand in all their glory and if our efforts can in

some way help turn the tide against the onslaught of the the foreign loggers and help the locals in their fight to keep their forests, then our ambitions will have been achieved.

Original Aim

The aim of our expedition was to survey the avifauna of the central plateau of the Nakanai mountain range of New Britain, off the coast of Papua New Guinea, with particular regard to the status of the island's restricted-range and endemic species as designated by Birdlife International.

Rationale

New Britain's avifauna has a high proportion of endemism (species found nowhere else) and the Nakanai mountains are largely unsurveyed. In the last decade mounting economic pressure has led to an increase in logging activity on New Britain, with extensive tracts of southern and western lowland forests removed. Difficult access and extreme weather in the Nakanais have so far kept the montane forests safe, but with technological advances and the huge demand for tropical hardwood, pristine forests may soon be in danger. Gathering information on the bird fauna of these forests should provide ammunition for the conservation of the Nakanai forests.
