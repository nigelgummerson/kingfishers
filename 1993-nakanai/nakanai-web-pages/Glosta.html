Cape Glosta

From the far east to the far west of New Britain: to Cape Glosta. We travelled by boat and light plane; the boat sank two days after we left it, sadly with the loss of eight lives. Our aim was to assist the West New Britain Provincial Government (WNBPG), who are trying to encourage small scale specialist tourism in New Britain as an aid to conservation of its natural resources. Marsha Bermann is at the forefront of this and she, together with the District Forestry Officer, is working hard to push for the creation of a reserve on the western slopes of Mount Talawe.

Mount Talawe dominates the Cape. It is an extinct volcano with a small regularly active cone by its side. The volcano's flanks are covered in forest, which extends right down to the coast. Virtually all of Glosta's primary forest has been signed over to the Lolo Development Corporation (LDC), a Malaysian logging company, and extensive logging has already occurred. Although we did not see logged areas, we were told how LDC has badly mismanaged the logging, with no replanting in many areas and soil erosion affecting freshwater supplies on which villages depend. This has caused a backlash from villagers, many of whom we spoke to are against the logging despite the lure of financial rewards.

The potential tourist attractions in the area are:-

  1. Numerous WWII relics, including planes and amphibious tanks;
  2. Active volcanoes;
  3. Traditional culture;
  4. Terrestrial Wildlife;
  5. Marine Wildlife;
  6. Catholic Mission at Kilenge (1929).

It was with a view to assess the bird life, with particular regard to the Cape's large hornbill population, that the expedition was invited to study two sites in the area.

Hornbill Point

Hornbill Point is a coastal peninsula roosting site for huge numbers of Blyth's Hornbills that fly down from the slopes of Mt. Talawe at dusk to roost and leave again at dawn. The peninsula is approximately 500m long and 70m across and projects from the western end of New Britain (5 degrees 33 minutes South 148 degrees 19 minutes East). Sagsag village is located at its base.

During our week on the point, we made several counts of hornbill numbers and also compiled a species list for the coastal area which can be found on page 80. We recorded eight restricted-range species on the Point, of which two are endemic to New Britain. Living on the beach had advantages: fresh fish and coconuts made a welcome change. However, the sand flies were particularly fond of our blood!

The hornbills flew in to roost between 17.30 and 19.00 and left again between 04.30 and 05.30. Our highest count revealed that at least 337 individuals roost on the point - this is a conservative estimate. Furthermore, local villagers informed us that on calmer days (throughout our stay there were strong southerly winds) there are many more birds. We had no way of confirming this claim but if it is true we could have greatly under-estimated the number of hornbills in the area. The majority of the roosting birds had yellow necks, identifying them as males or immatures; there were much lower numbers of mature females. As breeding females are sealed inside their nesting chamber by their mate for several weeks, it is our guess that the birds that roost on Hornbill Point are the non-breeding population (including immatures) plus the males of breeding pairs. However it was impossible to determine what proportion of the population were holed-up (and therefore uncounted) females.

We observed that the birds were flying in to roost from all directions. It was clear that there is no single area inland that is of paramount importance to the hornbills, but rather that the birds forage wherever there is suitable food - primarily in undisturbed rainforest. Obviously Cape Glosta has a sizable population of hornbills at present but any logging in this area will reduce this population. Eventually a critical population size must be reached below which the population will be too small to sustain itself; unfortunately it is impossible to predict what that minimum viable population size is.

Blyth's hornbills require large tree-holes in mature forests in which to nest and, being a timid bird, these must be located away from human disturbance. In addition to a nesting site each pair requires a large area of forest in which to forage. Therefore it is a fair assumption that extensive areas of primary forest must be preserved on the Cape if the large and important population of these birds is to be maintained.

Hornbill Point is a beautiful place: sandy beaches, a colourful reef and lush vegetation. It is also a very good birdwatching spot. The large numbers of hornbills are its star attraction, but there is also a lot to see offshore: hundreds of terns as well as frigate birds, ospreys fishing, etc; and there is a good selection of species in the forest too. The local villagers are very friendly and all in all it is a very relaxing site. The baby turtles swimming in an old oil drum, proudly shown to us by one villager, suggest that these creatures do still lay eggs nearby, thus adding to the Point's importance.

Whilst the point undoubtedly has the potential to be a major tourist attraction, disturbance to the site must be kept to a minimum as the welfare of the hornbills and other wildlife must take top priority. Too many visitors could ultimately destroy much of the sites attraction.

Mount Talawe

Inland, we were located between the villages of Aimola and Gie about 10km in from the coast at the western tip of New Britain, at an elevation of 1000m (5 degrees 35 minutes South 148 degrees 21 minutes East). Here we camped on a river-bank in a pocket of primary forest for two weeks, where we worked hard to compile a species list and collect population density data.

Our camp on Mount Talawe was simply idyllic. It was the river that made it, with its breath-taking waterfalls and plunge-pools surrounded in rich forest. We were taken to the river's source a little way upstream, a sacred place which we were told women were forbidden to enter. Whether this applies to tourists too was unclear, but it was quite the most magical place one could ever hope to experience.

The local people, the youngest of whom had not seen white men before, were always very helpful and friendly and we were able to get to know some of them very well. On the eve before we left, the village chapel choir sang to us and we had a feast and gift-giving ceremony. We were very sad to leave.

Before leaving New Britain we left a report of our findings and recommendations at Glosta with the WNBPG. We have since heard that the report will be part of a petition presented to the National Minister for Environment and Conservation and the LDC to try and curb the uncontrolled logging in New Britain.

Findings and Conclusions

Once inland, the expedition began by searching for areas of undisturbed primary forest, but unfortunately we were thwarted at this first step. Led by local villagers, we trekked through the concession area, only to find that the majority of it was degraded by extensive village agriculture. There were a few remnants of primary forest but nothing extensive. If there were any extensive areas within the logging extension area, we were not taken to them; this could be because the villagers never visit them themselves so there are no tracks there. However the high human population density suggests that it is more likely that the whole area is largely gardens and regrowth. From vantage points we could see that large areas of the Cape in general are still covered in primary forest, but we were confined to studying the avifauna of the gardens and secondary growth, with a few patches of primary forest, around Aimola.

Consequently the birding was not as exciting as it could have been. The mature forest around our camp contained a good number and variety of birds, including some (such as the Bismarks kingfisher) associated with the river. In the gardens and villages the birds were much less abundant and diverse. Many of the species that we recorded were only ever found in the pockets of primary forest. Other wildlife recorded in the area included two large monitor lizards, one of which was killed and eaten by our guides. The full list of bird species that we recorded at these sites, which includes 20 restricted-range species (4 of which are endemic to the island) can be found at Hornbill Point and Mount Talawe

Much larger areas of primary forest were clearly visible but apparently not accessible from our camp. This was frustrating as such areas undoubtedly hold rich bird faunas. It is also just such areas that the logging companies are interested in, but these primary forests must be preserved if the area is to retain a diverse fauna and hence much of its attraction to the tourist. To make the area more attractive to the visiting naturalist it would be necessary to ensure that trails are available which enter some of the larger tracts of undisturbed forest, preferably even to the top of Mount Talawe whose summit must offer some spectacular views.

Throughout our stay the villagers of Aimola and Gi were extremely hospitable and helpful. They were willing guides, regularly brought us food and of course there was the wonderful party they threw us on our last night. All in all they made sure that our stay with them was a truly memorable time and we can thoroughly recommend a stay at their village.

The area certainly has huge potential for small scale specialist tourism. A beautiful landscape, friendly people, traditional culture and the potential for great nature-watching, providing the primary forest can be preserved and parts of it made accessible. The village of Aimola even has some ancient rock inscriptions. Encouragement of tourism in the area is a very positive way of exploiting the valuable rainforest without destruction of the resource.

However, alone, tourism may not be enough. In order to preserve the full biodiversity of the Cape much greater expanses of forest need to be protected than the small areas targeted for tourism. Ideally the forests should be protected for their own sake and tourism would then be an important spin-off. Protection of any area of forest would have to be undertaken with the full understanding and consent of the villagers as it would require that they too kept disturbance of the area to a minimum. It would perhaps be necessary to set aside additional areas for village agriculture. However, the logging currently underway is not only irreversibly destroying the forest, but also the area's tourism potential and ultimately the lifestyle and livelihood of the villagers themselves.
