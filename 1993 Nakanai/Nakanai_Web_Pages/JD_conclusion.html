The Effects Of Land Use On Tropical Forest Bird Communities

Conclusions

The predictions made in the introduction are largely met by the observations. Not all the differences between the two sites are particularly striking, but all are noteworthy. If it was not for the pockets of primary forest at (and the extensive primary forest around) Talawe, the differences might be more marked.

Before human settlement on New Britain the two sites (Wild Dog and Talawe) were probably very similar in terms of habitat and bird communities. The habitat at Talawe has since been subject to considerable modification due to human land use. The primary rain forest has been degraded to gardens, regrowth and secondary forest, although a few pockets of primary forest remain.

Many specific niches have been lost from Talawe resulting in the disappearance of many species. The specialist carnivores are particularly susceptible compared with the vegetarians and generalist omnivores. Very few, if any, new species have colonised the new habitat, but many of the original species have adapted to the man-made habitat (ecological adaptation not evolutionary adaptation).

The loss of many specialised species and the new conditions created means that more resources are available for the birds that do remain. Some of the most specialised species may broaden their niches, becoming less specialised (which in turn will help them survive in the new habitat). The most noticeable beneficiaries are those species that are already generalists. Some generalist species are very abundant, usually those that are most adaptable (often those that are geographically widespread and successful urban colonisers). It follows that, other factors being equal, there will be fewer species existing at low densities. The reduction in species and the increase in the variation of abundance between species results in an overall lowering of diversity.

Restricted-range species in general do not appear to be any more susceptable to local extinction. A high proportion of the species at both Wild Dog and Talawe are r-r species. However, the majority of r-r species are specialist feeders and it appears that the r-r specialist carnivores are particularly threatened by disturbance.

Factors other than human disturbance may also contribute to the differences observed. Other variations between the two sites are minimal, e.g. they are both situated at about the same altitude, and we visited both at the same time of year in the same season. However, the two sites are at separate ends of New Britain island and some regional variations would exist. For example, ground doves are represented by Becarri's ground dove at Wild Dog and by Stephan's emerald dove at Talawe. It is hard to imagine, however, what other factors could cause the observed differences, which fit the predictions of disturbance factors so well.

Suggestions For Further Work

This project was very much a pilot study. Much more extensive investigations could be made into the effect of habitat disturbance on bird communities. The effect of habitat disturbance on other taxonomic groups could be the subject of an interesting study. Similar studies (of birds and other taxa) could be made at other locations to see whether the trends observed by this study are general. It would be interesting to quantify the habitat changes caused by land use and relate them to actual niches lost or gained. A long-term study could look at the change in a bird community of a primary rain forest as it underwent modification by land use.
