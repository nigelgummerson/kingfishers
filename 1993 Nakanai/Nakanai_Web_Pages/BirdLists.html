Site Lists

* = Birds caught in mist-nets.

Lake Hargy

Little grebeSeveral on the lake (at least 6). Often in pairs. Breeding and non-breeding plumages
Water whistling duck3 occasionally seen flying across the lake
OspreyA single observation of one fishing (first attempt successful)
Crested hawkOne observed in canopy on the way to the lake
Brahimny KiteOne seen drifting over canopy near lake
Melanesian scrub fowlOne flushed from ground on return trip from lake
White-browed crakeOne regularly observed in lake margin
Comb-crested jacanaOne regularly observed in margins and on floating vegetation
Red-knobbed fruit dove Regularly heard. Presumed to be in good numbers
White-bibbed fruit dove One female observed well. Others heard regularly
Torres Strait pigeon Recorded once, flying over canopy
Black imperial pigeonGroups of 5 to 20 birds occasionally seen flying high over forest, particularly at dusk.
No other pigeon species were positively identified but there undoubtedly were others; they were extremely difficult to observe at this site
Rainbow lorikeet Flocks very occasionally overhead
Eastern black-capped lory Occasionally overhead
Blue-eyed cockatoo Just one bird observed
Eclectus parrot Occasional pairs flying over
White-necked coucal One individual observed foraging in the canopy
New Britain bobook owl Calls of what we presumed were this species were heard at night, though not close to the lake
White-rumped swiftlet Good numbers of swiftlets, apparently all this species
Moustached tree-swift Small groups (of up to 7 birds) occasionally seen overhead
Bismarcks kingfisher* One seen on several occasions fishing from the lake
Dwarf kingfisher* Only observed when netted
Sacred kingfisher Individuals regularly perched around lake-side. Never observed fishing
Rainbow bee-eater One small group (7-8) recorded
Blyth's Hornbill Individuals or pairs regularly seen and heard in canopy and overhead
Northern fantail One or two birds occasionally seen feeding
Willie wagtail A pair frequently calling and singing by water's edge
Spangled drongo Several seen in canopy during trip to lake
Bismark pied monarch* Single individual observed once in mixed feeding flock
Shining flycatcher* Male netted; female observed feeding
Common golden whistler* Male netted; Pair observed feeding in forest during trip to lake
Ashy honeyeater* Single individuals occasionally observed
New Britain friarbird Common and very vocal
Black sunbird* Female netted. Individuals frequently observed
Yellow-faced myna Common and vocal
Torresian Crow Occasional individuals or pairs flying overhead

Wild Dog

New Britain buzzard Single individuals seen on 4-5 occasions
Brahimny Kite Single birds twice seen soaring above canopy
White-breasted sea eagle One immature observed flying over canopy
New Britain Grey-headed goshawk One bird seen once, perched in a tree
Oriental Hobby Two or three pairs in the area, suggesting a high density of these birds throughout the surrounding forest
Red-knobbed fruit dove The commonest fruit dove, regularly seen and heard
White-bibbed fruit dove* Also reasonably common
Torres Strait pigeon A common bird. Usually seen singly or in pairs and frequently heard 'singing'. Occasional larger flocks flying over, sometimes one or two birds seen in mixed pigeon flocks
Black imperial pigeon Another common bird though less regularly observed than the previous species. Usually seen in flocks of up to 30 birds flying high over the canopy or roosting at dusk
Finsch's imperial pigeon Recorded fairly regularly, usually individuals or pairs
Red-knobbed imperial pigeon Never positively recorded but probably present. Confusion with the preceding species
D'Albertis' mountain pigeon Small flocks, often in company of Black imperial pigeons
Brown cuckoo dove A very common bird. Usually in pairs. Very vocal
Pied cuckoo dove One bird briefly observed in flight
Beccari's ground dove* One individual flushed from the forest floor; two individuals netted
Rainbow lorikeet Quite regular noisy feeding flocks (up to 50 birds)
Eastern black-capped lory Regular in pairs or small flocks. One pair observed feeding with flock of rainbow lorikeets
Buff-faced pygmy parrot* A pair netted
Blue-eyed cockatoo Fairly frequently recorded, singly or in groups of up to four birds. Usually foraging in canopy and understorey
Eclectus parrot One pair seen in valley
Song parrot A fairly common bird. Usually seen in small groups (2-3). Two nest holes found in old trees, both with 5-6 birds in immediate vicinity. Very vocal
Shining bronze cuckoo Seen on 4 or 5 occasions, always singly, though sometimes in mixed-species feeding flock
White-necked coucal Occasionally heard duetting. Possibly Violaceous coucal also?
New Britain Bobook owl Calls, presumably of this species, heard every night, but the callers never found despite much searching
Glossy swiftletA common bird, particularly around the more open land-slide areas where up to 12 birds were sometimes present
Uniform swiftlet Large flocks occasionally seen wheeling overhead
White-rumped swiftlet Small numbers occasionally recorded
Moustached tree-swift A common bird, always to be seen perched trees or hawking overhead. Usually congregated in groups of up to 20 birds
Dwarf kingfisher* Three individuals netted
White-mantled kingfisher One pair recorded at the same site (perched in small trees beside the track) on several occasions
Sacred kingfisher Regularly seen perched in trees, frequently calling
Black-headed paradise kingfisher* One immature bird netted
Rainbow bee-eater Occasional flocks of up to 30 birds. Large numbers at lower altitude (on drive to and from the site)
Dollarbird Two birds recorded on one occasion
Blyth's Hornbill Frequently seen (and heard), usually pairs, sometimes small flocks flying over
White-bellied cuckoo-shrike Pairs seen regularly
Yellow-eyed cuckoo-shrike Occasionally recorded, usually singly
Cicadabird Common, singly and in small groups
Varied triller Infrequent but not uncommon, singly, pairs or small groups
Rufous-faced thicket warbler* One netted
Bismark rufous fantail* Single individuals seen occasionally. Two netted
Northern fantail* Regularly observed; usually singly occasionally in pairs
Spangled drongo* Quite common, in groups of 1 to 4. One netted
Bismark pied monarch* Infrequently observed but probably quite common. Three individuals netted. One pair observed noisily chasing a Northern fantail
Common golden whistler* Occasional sightings. Two individuals netted (both females)
Ashy honeyeater* Very common, usually singly or in pairs. Regularly netted
Red-tinted honeyeater Quite frequent, usually singly, occasionally in pairs
New Britain red-headed honeyeater Frequent, usually singly, occasionally in pairs. Often seen calling repeatedly from the tip of a twig
New Britain friarbird* Very common. Very vocal. Singly or in pairs
Black sunbird* Quite common. Usually seen singly, sometimes in mixed feeding flocks
Bismark flowerpecker Recorded infrequently but probably not uncommon
Black-headed white-eye Reasonably common; often in small feeding flocks, sometimes with other species
Singing starling Uncommon. 1-2 birds seen once or twice
Metallic starling Common in feeding flocks of up to 50 birds
Yellow-faced myna Very common. Almost invariably in pairs
White-backed wood-swallow 4-5 birds observed once in valley below
Torresian Crow Common, singly or in small groups

Hornbill Point

Lesser frigatebird Individuals and small groups regularly seen offshore and flying over the point. Often observed chasing terns
Eastern reef egret One or two birds sometimes present on the reef
Nankeen night heron One adult observed on the point at dusk
Osprey Up to at least five birds regularly around the point; possibly breeding
Brahimny Kite One or two birds regularly seen soaring over the forest
Whimbrel At least four birds regularly feeding on the reef
Common sandpiper A common bird on the shore and reef, singly or in pairs
Grey-tailed tattler One individual present on the point for a day or two
Common tern Very occasional individuals offshore
Black-naped tern One individual recorded amongst roost of noddies and crested terns on the reef
Bridled tern Small numbers regular offshore, sometimes roosting on the reef
Crested tern A very common bird. Large numbers (hundreds) offshore, on the reef and overhead
White-capped noddy As for crested tern. One individual observed sitting on the beach and a small group in the same place after dusk
Red-knobbed fruit dove Occasional birds in the forest
Torres Strait pigeon Small flocks and at least one nest on the point with four adults around it
Grey imperial pigeon Small flocks regular
Rainbow lorikeet Feeding flocks of up to 40 birds
Red-flanked lorikeet Occasional small feeding flocks (up to 20 birds)
Blue-eyed cockatoo Occasional individuals or small groups
Eclectus parrot Regular in small numbers
Uniform swiftlet Large numbers usually present. In flocks of up to 50 birds
Moustached tree-swift Small numbers always present
River kingfisher A pair regularly observed fishing on the shore
Sacred kingfisher A few individuals always present
Collared kingfisher One individual seen briefly
Blyth's Hornbill Huge numbers roost at night. Maximum count 337 birds and the locals assured us there are many more in less windy conditions. A few individuals sometimes remained during the day
White-bellied cuckoo-shrike Small groups (2-3) fairly regular
Varied triller Occasional individuals or pairs
Willie wagtail 2-3 pairs always present
Shining flycatcher Occasional individuals
Ashy honeyeater Quite common in small numbers
New Britain red-headed honeyeater Regular individually or in pairs
New Britain friarbird Common and vocal
Black sunbird Regular in small numbers
Yellow-bellied sunbird Infrequent, individually or in pairs
Bismark flowerpecker Very occasional individuals
Black-headed white-eye Small groups recorded infrequently
Metallic starling A common bird in large flocks (up to 100 birds)
Yellow-faced myna Common. A few pairs always present
Torresian Crow Common. Often in small groups

Mount Talawe

Nankeen night heron One immature in flight at dawn
Crested hawk One record, in secondary forest
Brahimny Kite Single birds often seen soaring above the canopy
Variable goshawk* Single birds seen quite regularly in all forest types
Melanesian scrub fowl One individual flushed from thick brush on forest floor
Common sandpiper Seen once, flying along the river
Red-knobbed fruit dove Recorded only once, but probably more common than this would suggest
Torres Strait pigeon A common bird. Often in flocks of up to 40 individuals
Black imperial pigeon Single birds or small groups occasionally seen in flight
Red-knobbed imperial pigeon Regularly seen in pairs or groups, feeding in canopy
Finsch's imperial pigeon Rare or absent. Confusion with preceding species
Stephan's emerald dove* Individuals regularly flushed from the more open areas of forest floor
Rainbow lorikeet Very common. Large noisy flocks
Eastern black-capped lory Common in small parties. One nest hole found about 15m up in a large dead tree
Red-chinned lorikeet Small feeding flocks quite regular, especially in early morning
Red-flanked lorikeet As for preceding species with which this species regularly associated
Blue-eyed cockatoo Common and vocal. In groups of up to 10 birds. One nest hole found
Eclectus parrot Regularly seen in pairs
Song parrot Uncommon, in small vocal groups
Channel-billed cuckoo Twice seen flying over canopy
Violaceous coucal Individuals seen twice in primary forest. Pairs occasionally heard duetting
White-necked coucal Quite common in all forest types. Groups of up to 6 birds. Regularly heard duetting
New Britain bobook owl Heard one night in the village (presumably this species)
Glossy swiftlet Small flock seen once along the river
Moustached tree-swift Common in all habitats
River kingfisher One or two birds occasionally seen along river
Bismarcks kingfisher One individual recorded once, fishing in river
Dwarf kingfisher* Occasionally seen singly along the river
White-mantled kingfisher One pair seen perched in low bush next to road
Sacred kingfisher Common in gardens and secondary forest
Rainbow bee-eater Small parties quite common in gardens and more open forest
Blyth's Hornbill Regular in pairs and small groups, especially in primary forest
White-bellied cuckoo-shrike Regular in all habitats; individuals and pairs
Varied triller Groups of 1-4 birds regular in forest
Northern fantail* Occasionally observed in forest habitats
Spangled drongo Common in all habitats. Usually in small groups (up to 6 birds)
Bismark pied monarch* Recorded once in primary forest
Shining flycatcher Single individuals regular in forest and forest edge
Ashy honeyeater* A common forest bird, individually or in small groups
New Britain red-headed honeyeater Quite frequently recorded in forest; individuals or pairs
New Britain friarbird Very common and vocal in all habitats
Black sunbird Individuals regular in all habitats
Bismark flowerpecker Quite common in primary forest
Black-headed white-eye Quite common in small flocks in forest
Singing starling Small groups quite common, particularly in disturbed habitat
Metallic starling Flocks of 1-15 birds quite common in all habitats
Torresian Crow Common in small groups in all habitats
