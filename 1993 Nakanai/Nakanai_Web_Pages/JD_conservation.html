The Effects Of Land Use On Tropical Forest Bird Communities

Restricted-range Species: Conservation

40% of the species recorded at Wild Dog are r-r species, almost exactly the same proportion (42%) of the birds at Talawe are r-r. Thus it would appear that r-r species are no more susceptible to local extinction caused by human disturbance than are other species. Our prediction that there would be proportionately fewer r-r species at Talawe is not borne out.

However, it is interesting to look at the r-r species with respect to the niches they exploit (the diet categories). A greater proportion (48%) of the specialist feeders (vegetarians and carnivores: diets 1-5) are r-r species compared with omnivores (29%). Amongst the omnivores, three out of four nectar+insect-eating species - quite a specialist group - are r- r species. The fact that carnivores seem particularly susceptible to local extinction has already been discussed; the trend is particularly evident amongst the r-r species. All but one of the eight r-r species present at Wild Dog but not Talawe are specialised carnivores. In comparison only one of the four r-r species at Talawe but not Wild Dog is a carnivore, the others being a generalist and two vegetarians. It is clear that carnivorous r-r species are particularly threatened by human habitat disturbance.

Their very definition implies that restricted range species are particularly vulnerable to global extinction. There are a large number of r-r species on New Britain, some of them confined entirely to this one island. Many are considered globally threatened, see Collar & Andrew ('88) and Johnson & Stattersfield ('90) . Thus it is a site of primary conservation importance - if birds are lost from this island they are lost globally.

As for the actual sites, Wild Dog has a very rich bird community and would be very valuable as a reserve - kept free from human disturbance. Talawe does not have such a diverse community; even so, it does support quite a wide range of species many of which are restricted- range and some of which are not found at Wild Dog. It is important to understand that the Talawe location is not intrinsically poorer than the Wild Dog area; the primary forest that still remains intact on the cape is just as valuable as the habitat at Wild Dog. To preserve the full diversity of species on New Britain a number of protected areas are needed throughout the island to encompass all regional variation and the protected areas should be kept free of human disturbance, including local land use.
