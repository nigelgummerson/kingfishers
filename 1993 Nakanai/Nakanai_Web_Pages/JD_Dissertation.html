The Effects Of Land Use On Tropical Forest Bird Communities

Abstract

Two mid-montane tropical rain forest sites are compared to assess whether differences in habitat, caused by extensive human disturbance at one of the sites, are reflected in their bird communities. Species lists were compiled for each site and spot counts were made to calculate densities. The lists were subdivided according to the diets of the birds, to give an indication of the birds distribution among niche types.

The results suggest a significant difference between the bird communities of the two sites. The disturbed site has fewer species, with a reduction in all diet types except for vegetarians, which appear not to have been affected, and true generalists, which have actually done better. A few of the common species at the disturbed site are particularly abundant (these are mostly generalists) whilst it has much fewer scarce species, so overall equitability and diversity is lower than in the non-disturbed site. Specialist carnivorous restricted-range species are particularly vulnerable to local extinction, whilst other r-r species are no more susceptable than are more widespread species. The implications of this study to tropical bird conservation are briefly discussed

Aim

To assess whether the habitat differences between two forest sites on New Britain, one primary forest (Wild Dog) and the other largely secondary forest and gardens (Mount Talawe), are reflected in the composition of their bird communities.

Introduction: The Effects of Land Use

Wherever villages are located in tropical rain forest, the surrounding forest tends to be cut down to make space for crop gardens. The gardens are shifted periodically: a new area is cleared and the old garden left to regenerate (See Feyne & McAlpine 1985; Collins et al, 1991, ch.4). If left to regenerate without any further disturbance the cleared site may still take hundreds of years to revert to true primary forest. Sometimes regeneration will never occur, particularly if the cleared area is very large. In the meantime the habitat goes through a succession of regrowth and secondary forest. If the site is periodically cleared it will be kept in an early to mid- successional state. These new habitats (gardens, regrowth and secondary forest) lack many of the features of primary forest, such as the structurally complex, high canopy in which many of the forest denizens live (see Mabberly 1992). Other features are created, such as dense under-storey, open space and forest edge. Regeneration and succession after clearance are not fully understood, but good discussions can be found in Hopkins 1990, Marshall & Swaine 1992, Reiners 1992 and Gomez-Pompa et al 1991.

Such habitat changes cause the loss of many niches for which forest birds are specialised. Unless the species are able to adapt to a new ecology they will be driven extinct. Many species are not able to adapt; Davis 1993, for example, found that at his study site in Bolivia more than half of the forest species were never recorded in human-altered habitat. The replacement of old niches by new ones does not result in an equal number of new species replacing those driven extinct. There are two reasons for this discrepancy.

(1) The new habitats are invariably less diverse and complex: the vegetation consists of a portion, but not the whole, of the previous ecosystem, plus a few introduced or invasive species; the habitat is more homogenous, with fewer micro habitats. Thus there are fewer niches available. See McWilliam 1993 and Smiet 1992 for recent studies of vegetational changes caused by human disturbance
(2) Replacement by a new species requires either colonisation by, or the evolution of, the new species. Evolution is a very slow process, whilst colonisation is a rare event. Few species are specialist colonisers. There just are not large numbers of specially-adapted species waiting to move into a man-made habitat as soon as it is created (See Diamond, 1973).

The birds that do colonise the new habitat, or that remain in the area despite the disturbance, are usually generalists, or opportunists - birds that are not adapted for a highly specific lifestyle, but are instead able to adapt their behaviour to a new environment. Also, generalists tend to be better adapted for colonisation: wide dispersal, etc. These generalists are also the common birds of towns in the region. Due to the lack of competition from specialists, successful colonists may become very abundant.

In contrast, the birds of undisturbed tropical rain forests have had time to adapt and diversify to fill specific niches. Most are thus highly specialised. Indeed, the high species diversity of tropical rain forests is largely due to the partitioning of the ecosystem into many highly specific niches. As a result most species exist at relatively low densities (most species are "scarce") (See Dinerstein & Wikramanayake 1993). A large proportion of these scarce, specialised species are absent from disturbed forest (See Beehler 1985).

The abundance of a few common species and the lack of many scarce species means that the community has a low species diversity. Diversity is a measure of species richness (number of species) combined with equitability. Equitability is the eveness of the distribution of individuals between the different species in the community. The most equitable community is one in which all the species exist at the same density. Undisturbed rainforest has a high number of species, all at fairly low densities, and is thus highly diverse. A disturbed site will have fewer species, a few of which will account for most of the individuals; thus disturbed sites will have lower diversity.

Many of these specialised species have a limited geographical range. They are "restricted-range" (r-r) species. It follows that r-r species will be particularly vulnerable to habitat disturbance. There are likely to be proportionately fewer r-r species in disturbed habitat than in virgin forest (see Diamond 1975 and also Rickart 1993).

In addition to the physical removal of vegetation, the continual presence of humans and their domesticated animals (mainly dogs and pigs in the New Guinea region) scares away the more timid species. Other species may disappear because they feed on prey that is "timid" (particularly small vertebrates which are hunted actively by people and dogs). Timid bird species may become extinct in a disturbed area, not because their niche is destroyed, but instead because they cannot tolerate human presence.

To sum up: human disturbance of a rain forest is likely to cause:-

  1. A reduction in total number of species, particularly scarce and restricted-range species
  2. A decrease in the variety of niches occupied
  3. The increased abundance of a few common species, which are likely to be opportunists/generalists
  4. 1 & 3 = A decrease in diversity

This project investigates whether these predictions are borne out in the field.

Similar studies have been done by Driscoll '77, who found that conifer plantations in New Zealand held fewer species of birds (but an equal number of, or more, individuals) than native forest; Zhijun '91, who found fewer species of birds in logged forest than in undisturbed forest in China; and Fenton et al '92, who discovered a decrease in the diversity of bat species in disturbed rain forest habitats.

Some studies have shown that limited disturbance may actually increase species diversity by creating a greater variety of conditions without destroying much of the original habitat. An example is Bell '80, and the idea is discussed by Connell '78.

Due to the dearth of ornithological exploration on New Britain very little is known about the island's birds, in particular about their ecology. Diet is a very important factor in determining a bird's niche, and is known for many of New Britain's birds (from examination of the stomach contents of collected birds and from field observation). Thus the ecology of the birds can be examined by partitioning them on the basis of their diet (Fenton et al '92 applied a similar technique to Pyllostomid bats). Each diet category subsumes numerous potential niches.
