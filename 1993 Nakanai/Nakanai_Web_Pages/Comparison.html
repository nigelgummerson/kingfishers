A Comparison between Wild Dog and Mount Talawe

In addition to the general observations made at Wild Dog and Mount Talawe, the team conducted a series of "spot-counts" at the two sites, from which population density estimates could be made for the commoner species. This data was not accurate enough to be of much value in assessing actual population densities, but it does enable comparisons to be made between sites and it was used in this way, along with the species lists, by Jon Clay in his finals project. An edited version of that project is reproduced HERE. The full report can be found in the Zoology Department library, Oxford.

The project compares the bird community of Wild Dog with that of Mount Talawe, attempting to correlate differences between the two communities with differences in human disturbance at the two sites. As noted earlier, human disturbance has significantly modified the habitat we studied at Mount Talawe, compared with Wild Dog where the habitat is still very much as it must have been before humans colonised New Britain. At Wild Dog the birds occupy the niches that they evolved to fill, whereas at Mount Talawe niches have been modified, lost and created. The question is: Is the difference in land degradation and human disturbance between the two sites reflected in their bird communities?
