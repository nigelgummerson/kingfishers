Conservation Proposals

The original aim of this expedition was to collect data on the fauna of the Nakanais that would confirm the mountains to be biologically important, and that would support proposals that the area be declared a national park. Although we failed in our data collection, we remain convinced that the Nakanais support a diverse and unique fauna that is worthy of special protection.

Beehler (1993b) lists the Nakanai Range as one of the 14 top priority (Priority One) Biologically Important Areas in Papua New Guinea. He describes the area as follows:

"another of PNG's great wilderness areas - uninhabited and unsurveyed. It is certain to be biologically rich, and there is little doubt that taxonomically new birds and mammals inhabit this area."
The Area includes lowland forest in the interior of the southern watershed.

Two other Biologically Important Areas have been identified on New Britain. One of them, "Mount Sinewit/Mount Biririnja", encompasses the highlands of the Gazelle Peninsula, including Wild Dog. It is described as follows:

"These mountains, isolated by rivers and lowlands from the Nakanai Mountains to the southwest, are certainly as fascinating as the latter. They have not been adequately surveyed, and are 500m higher. These mountains are surrounded by lowlands with a growing populace, and probably will be degraded unless action is taken soon."

The other Area is the Whiteman Range:

"This area comprises the the highest range in central New Britain and an expanse of hill and lowland forest in north and south of the range... "
"This area includes the largest single selection of PNG species endemics of any in this list [of Biologically Important Areas in PNG]. Pteropus gilliardi is known only from this area, and is perhaps indicative of the area's unsurveyed richness. It is very little studied and merits special attention, especially when considering the potential effects of the planned timber development projects in the region."

Elsewhere, Beehler (1993a) identifies other areas of high biological diversity that merit special attention. These include two wetlands: the Aria Wetlands on the northern coast of West New Britain, and the Toriu Wetlands on the eastern coast of the Gazelle Peninsula; and the Willaumez Peninsula:

"A remarkable physiographic feature with Lake Dakataua, it includes a very diverse area of lowland rain forest on rich volcanic soils. Threatened by logging and proposed development for oil palm plantations."

The Biologically Important Areas described by Beehler were drawn up by an international team of scientists, the aim being to identify the areas of PNG where conservation efforts should be targeted. Beehler suggests that

"the Department of Environment and Conservation (DEC) should focus on developing a system of large conservation areas to serve as a biological reserve for the nation"
Ideally, each Biologically Important Area, or as large a portion of it as is possible, should be designated as a National Park (or Forest Reserve) and given full protection. The Reserves should be locally owned, where applicable, (i.e. remain in the ownership of the traditional landowners), but administered with the assistance of the DEC.

Thus, in New Britain there should be at least three Forest Reserves, encompassing the Nakanai Mountains, the Whiteman Range and the Gazelle Highlands. These areas are largely uninhabited, or at the most, sparsely populated; indeed, the former two have been identified as two of PNG's Major Wilderness Areas. This should make their designation as conservation areas relatively easy. Conservation efforts should not, however, be restricted to wilderness areas. It is vitally important that the people of PNG are taught about conservation issues and given the means and the incentive to put this into practice.

New environmental legislation is needed that is simple to understand and simple to enforce. Local conservation efforts will only be effective if they have the approval of the landowners involved. There should be explicit incentives (non-cash aid) for the adoption of environmentally sound practices by traditional landowners. Furthermore, such practices should be actively promoted.

The biggest threat to the natural environment of New Britain (and the rest of PNG) is deforestation, largely by foreign logging companies, but also to make way for plantations, mining operations, and even local agriculture. The problem is not just the irretrievable loss of the island's diverse and unique wildlife; New Britain's forests are an extremely valuable resource and they are being recklessly squandered, for relatively small short-term profits. Once the forest has been cleared, soil is quickly eroded, waterways become silted up, and the landowners are left with nothing but wasteland. If properly managed, however, the forests can provide the landowners with substantial profits, for generation after generation, and with minimal environmental degradation.

The tragedy is that, too often, traditional landowners are lured by false promises into selling timber rights to foreign logging companies, without any knowledge of the devastating effects that will follow. They are promised roads, bridges, perhaps a school and a hospital and are won over with cash hand-outs and crates of beer. Rarely, however, does the promised infrastructure ever materialise, even the roads necessary for the logging operations quickly fall into disrepair once the loggers have gone and of course all cash-flow immediately stops. The landowners are left with no cash, a crumbling infrastructure, and they have lost their most valuable asset - the forest.

There are alternatives. The forests can generate an income for their owners either directly, through sustainable harvesting of forest products, or indirectly through tourism.

Sustainable Harvesting

The most valuable forest commodity that can be harvested on a large scale is, of course, timber. Timber really can be harvested on a sustainable basis, without the destruction of the resource, as is being demonstrated by the Pacific Heritage Foundation's Riet TA Project.

The Riet Project is described as follows:

"The villagers will be encouraged to use their forest as a sustainable resource, and the trees which are cut will be converted into timber by the landowners themselves..."
It is considered that they will [therefore] be more confident in their ability to resist outside attempts to gain control of their land.
"By processing their own timber the villagers can obtain far greater income than they would by allowing export logging. They have a strong vested interest in ensuring that the forest stays in good condition. Their continued wealth depends on maintaining the forest, and the Project will demonstrate to villagers in other areas that there is a viable and practical alternative to export logging."
Henderson & Thomas, 1993.

Harvesting is carried out using small, portable sawmills; the timber is then sawn and kiln dried before it is exported. The export market for the timber, essential to the project's success, was initially established by the Ecological Trading Company (ETC) in the UK and now has the support of major British retailers. The project currently has orders that exceed production capacity.

Max Henderson, founder of the Riet Project, writes about the potential for sustainable logging in PNG:

"It is generally accepted that our types of forest can sustain a harvesting rate at an average of 1 cubic metre per hectare per year. If we consider that there are 15,000,000 ha. of accessible forest [in PNG], then a managed and controlled rate of harvesting of 15,000,000 c.m. of logs per year would be feasible, ad infinitum.

That would give an annual income to Papua New Guinea of K3.1 billion, or nearly K8000 for every man, woman and child, placing us among the the highest levels of per-capita income in the world. And this could happen whilst our greatest resource was fully protected."

Despite such strong arguments, Papua New Guinea continues to virtually give away its forests to foreign logging companies who blatantly abuse what regulations there are, deceive the land-owners on whose land they are operating, dodge the taxes, and run off with the spoils leaving behind nothing but a wasteland. The forestry industry is rife with corruption and malpractice and remains so thanks to bribery and deceit. The situation must change, and time is running out.

Traditional landowners need to be informed about the current logging situation. It is vital that they understand the consequences of selling and losing their forests, and that they are aware of the alternatives available to them. The export of logs should be banned; the minimum processing requirement for the granting of an Export Permit should be sawn and kiln dried timber.

Papua New Guinea still has most of its forests, but almost half of them have already been signed over to foreign loggers. These logging rights should be withdrawn; PNG's biodiversity should be conserved within a series of large Forest Reserves, and the rest of the forests should be managed on a sustainable basis, and by traditional landowners. This way, Papua New Guinea will keep its greatest asset, will remain one of the most beautiful countries on Earth, and will become one of the richest. It makes sense.

Tourism

It has been predicted that tourism will be the largest industry, globally, by the year 2000. Tropical rainforests are a major tourist attraction and, what is more, tourism is not incompatible with sustainable timber harvesting.

Marsha Berman of the West New Britain Tourist Bureau is using tourism very effectively as a means of securing protection for sites of special interest and natural heritage sites. In 1992 the W.N.B. Provincial Government passed the Tourist Sites Protection Act which

"gives the landowners the tool to stop people who cause disturbances to sites of significance".
Proposed sites range from traditional taboo sites to lakes and rivers, reefs, forest areas, and WWII wrecks. Under the act, a number of wildlife parks have been proposed, including Lake Hargy and Hornbill Point.

As part of the Act, the Tourist Bureau is implementing a "Four Year Plan for Controlled Tourism Development"; this involves the promotion of the province as a destination for special interest visitors, such as scientists, fishermen and bird-watchers. Most importantly, however, the Plan provides for the building of guest houses to be operated by the local villagers, and the training of staff for the houses and guides to take the visitors on tours. Thus, local villagers are to earn an income that is entirely dependent upon the well-being of their scenic surroundings, including lakes and forests.

It is projects like Max's in the Bainings and Marsha's in West New Britain that are most likely to succeed in preserving the natural habitats of New Britain. Working at grassroots level, they aim to convince local people of the value of their natural heritage and, by providing them with an income that is dependent upon that heritage, they provide the villagers with an incentive to conserve their natural environment.
