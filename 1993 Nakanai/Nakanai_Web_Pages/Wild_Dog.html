Wild Dog

A traumatic eighteen hour boat ride found the expedition in Rabaul, at the tip of East New Britain. We were there in response to an invitation from Max Henderson of the Pacific Heritage Foundation. The Foundation's aim is to demonstrate a realistic alternative to the current practise of clear-felling large areas of forest for short-term profit. That alternative is small-scale, village-based logging which allows the forest to regenerate and is therefore sustainable indefinitely. We were requested to assess the bird life of an area of forest that Max hoped to preserve through his sustainable logging project.

Background and location

The Bainings people are the original occupants of the entire Gazelle Peninsula; however, the sale of their land has recently become their main source of income. Now, the Bainings people are largely restricted to the highland areas of the peninsula. Once sold, the land is usually cleared of its forests to make a quick profit from its timber and/or to make room for agriculture. To prevent this widescale deforestation, the Bainings people need an incentive to keep hold of their land: a means of making money from it other than through its outright sale.

The Pacific Heritage Foundation's Riet Community-Based Ecoforestry Project (Riet TA Project) provides the villagers of Riet with an income by enabling them to harvest their own forests. The villagers are being taught to do so in a way that does not destroy those forests: by the selective logging of individual trees within designated plots. The plots are then left to regenerate before any further logging continues. The amount of timber to be harvested each year has been based on the rate of regeneration that the forest is capable of. Furthermore, by processing the harvested timber before exporting it, its value will be dramatically increased.

The next stage of the project will be to purchase the equipment for the manufacture of finished products and the training of workers and tradespeople. Max estimates that once this stage is reached, only a tiny fraction (some 2-3%) of the volume of timber exported by clear-felling operations on a similar sized area of land will need to be harvested to generate the same income for the landowners.

The Riet Project aims to harvest timber from 1200 of the 2800 hectares owned by the Riet clan. A further 1000 hectares is to be left untouched in forest reserves. "Wild Dog" is the site of an old mining exploration camp and is within one of the areas to be set aside as a reserve. Our expedition was invited to survey the birds of the forest at Wild Dog, to assess the area's biological worth.

The Riet Project is a pilot project; it aims to demonstrate to other clans the huge benefits of landowner-controlled timber harvesting and processing. In time, many other villages should be persuaded to follow Riet's example, instead of selling their land to foreign logging companies. One of the Pacific Heritage Foundation's aims is to set up a 30,000 hectare reserve in the Gazelle highlands by persuading the landowners of the value of their resource and of the need to preserve it. The Wild Dog Reserve is to be a part of this larger Bainings Reserve.

We based ourselves at the mining camp which proved to be relatively luxurious: solidly constructed sheds, drinking water on tap and even a wood-stove. It is located in the Bainings Mountains, (4 degrees 37 minutes South, 152 degrees 02 minutes East) at an altitude of 1050 m above sea-level.

The camp was being maintained in good order because the miners plan to return soon and embark on a commercial mining operation. This would undoubtedly have a devastating effect on the surrounding forest. We are unsure what control the villagers of Riet have over the mining project; we can only stress that mining is not compatible with forest preservation and would most likely have far-reaching effects on the forest community. The commencement of mining should be prevented if at all possible, however it is a small threat compared with clear-fell logging which could denude most of the Gazelle Peninsula (indeed, most of Papua New Guinea).

Findings and Conclusions

The exploratory mining operations, mainly carried out in 1991, have so far been fairly limited in extent, being confined to the top of a steep sided mountain ridge. All around is virgin rainforest. The miners have cut one or two major tracks which still remain along the main ridge, and numerous other cuttings which crisscross the top of the ridge but have now largely been overgrown. Even this limited disturbance, however, has caused a number of land slips on the steep slopes directly below.

Due to the steepness of the slopes we were largely confined to the top of the ridge, but the mining tracks made access along the ridge easy and large areas of primary forest on the slopes below could be observed. Consequently we were able to compile a considerable species list for the area and we have little doubt that the bird community is even richer in the virgin forest away from the mining operations.

We recorded a total of 56 bird species during the course of our stay at Wild Dog; 22 of these are restricted-range species, including 7 of the 12 species endemic to New Britain and 4 of the 10 New Britain species considered to be near-threatened. See "Observations"

Wild Dog undoubtedly supports a very rich bird community whose diversity can probably match that of any other area of forest on the island. It holds many species that are not found outside the Bismarcks, or indeed outside New Britain. Furthermore, a few of the species that we recorded had previously only ever been recorded at lower altitudes; it seems that at Wild Dog typically lowland species were existing side-by-side with the highland species making for a very unique and diverse assemblage of birds. Add to this the fact that the area holds at least four species that need very careful monitoring, as they are likely to soon become threatened with extinction, and it becomes clear that the Wild Dog Reserve is an area of great ornithological importance, and eminently worthy of preservation.

Birds aside, the area was also rich in other wildlife: wallabies, several species of bats, mice, snakes, frogs and a huge variety of invertebrates. Furthermore, the place really was incredibly beautiful. Our findings at Wild Dog indicate that the whole of the Bainings area is biologically very rich. It would be a tragedy to lose all this due to inadequate logging operations, especially as there are real alternatives.

We commend the work of the Pacific Heritage Foundation and wish Max every success in his battle to safeguard the forests of New Britain through the setting up of sustainable logging projects.
