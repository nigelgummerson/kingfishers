# New Britain Kingfisher (*Todiramphus albonotatus*)

*Compiled 2026-02-17*

## Taxonomy

- **Current accepted name:** *Todiramphus albonotatus* (Ramsay, E.P., 1884)
- **Former name:** *Halcyon albonotata* — the name current during the 1993 fieldwork
- **Common names:** New Britain Kingfisher (eBird, Birds of the World); White-mantled Kingfisher (IOC)
- **Family:** Alcedinidae, subfamily Halcyoninae
- **Monotypic** — no subspecies

### Taxonomic history

- Originally described by Edward Pierson Ramsay (Curator of the Australian Museum, 1874–1894) as *Halcyon (Cyanalcyon) albonotata* in *Proceedings of the Linnean Society of New South Wales*, 9: 863 (1884). Type locality: New Britain.
- Transferred to *Todiramphus* following Woodall (2001) in the *Handbook of the Birds of the World*, supported by Moyle (2006) molecular phylogeny demonstrating *Halcyon* sensu lato was not monophyletic.
- The name *albonotatus* derives from Latin: *albus* (white) + *notatus* (marked), referring to the white dorsal plumage.

---

## Description

- **Length:** 16–18 cm
- **Bill:** Black
- **Male:** Mostly white body with blue wings, blue tail, turquoise/pale blue crown, thick black eye-stripe. White rump.
- **Female:** Similar but with blue on lower back/rump rather than white.
- Distinctive within its range — no confusion species on New Britain.

---

## Distribution and Habitat

- **Endemic to New Britain** (~36,500 km²), Papua New Guinea. Not recorded from any other island.
- Found across the island but concentrated in forested areas, sea level to ~1,000 m elevation.
- **Primary habitat:** Tropical moist lowland forest (forest interior).
- Also recorded in degraded forest, secondary forest, forest edges, and clearings.
- Forest dependency considered high.

---

## Population

- **Estimated population:** 2,500–10,000 mature individuals (BirdLife International precautionary estimate)
- **Trend:** Suspected declining, driven by lowland habitat loss
- **No formal census** has been conducted specifically for this species
- Consistently described as "widespread but scarce" in the literature
- Davis *et al.* (2018): 415 hours of surveys across New Britain recorded very few individuals

---

## Conservation Status

- **Current IUCN status:** Near Threatened (NT)
- **Recommended uplisting:** Davis *et al.* (2018) recommended reclassification to **Vulnerable** under criterion C2a(ii)

### Threats

**Oil palm expansion (primary threat):**
- 12% of New Britain's forest cleared between 1989–2000 (~3,000 km², 1.1% per year)
- Nearly 25% of all forest below 100 m altitude lost in this period
- Palm oil plantations expanded by 72.3% between 1989–2013
- New Britain Palm Oil Limited (NBPOL) and others driving large-scale lowland conversion
- West New Britain province is the centre of PNG's oil palm industry
- If pre-2000 deforestation rates continued, all forest below 200 m would be cleared by 2060

**Logging:**
- Open Bay Timber Limited (Sumitomo Forestry Group), Stettin Bay Lumber Company
- Deforestation increases along logging roads
- 2001–2020: 10% tree cover loss in parts of New Britain, ~60% of that in primary forest

**Current situation:**
- Rate of forest loss slowed after 2000 (~2.2% loss 2002–2014)
- As of 2014, 72% of New Britain remained forested (including secondary)
- Much forest remains at higher altitudes; lowland forest disproportionately impacted
- Species' dependence on lowland primary forest places it directly in the path of oil palm

---

## Ecology

### Diet
- No detailed dietary studies published.
- By analogy with other forest *Todiramphus*: insects, small lizards, terrestrial invertebrates. The genus feeds largely on terrestrial prey away from water.

### Breeding
- **Very poorly known.** No published accounts of nest, eggs, clutch size, or incubation.
- Other *Todiramphus* species: tree cavities or excavated termite mounds, both sexes share incubation, 2–6 white eggs, ~19–21 days incubation. Likely similar but unconfirmed.

### Vocalisations
- Rapid descending "kee-ku-ko-ko" or trilling "ki-ki-ki-ki-ki"
- Vocalisations are the primary detection method in the field.

### Behaviour
- Sedentary (non-migratory), as expected for an island endemic.
- Encountered singly or in pairs.
- Shy; often detected by call before being seen.

## Nakanai '93 Expedition

**Nakanai '93** was an Oxford University expedition to New Britain in 1993, funded by a **Future Conservationist Award** (the predecessor to the Conservation Leadership Programme, originally the BP Conservation Programme).

### Expedition details
- **Original objective:** Survey of the montane avifauna of the Nakanai Mountain range
- **What happened:** Access problems forced abandonment of the original plan. In collaboration with the **Pacific Heritage Foundation** and the **West New Britain Provincial Government**, alternative projects were identified.
- **Actual work:** Bird surveys, sound recordings, and population studies at three sites under threat from exploitation:
  1. **Lake Hargy** — at the base of the Nakanai range
  2. **The Bainings**
  3. **Cape Glosta area** — near Mount Talawe

### Team members
- **Jonathan Clay** — expedition leader
- **Nigel Gummerson** — team member (ornithology, kingfisher population study)
- **Nick Mason** — team member (subsequently went to work for the RSPB)
- **Jamie** — team member (surname and subsequent career unknown)

### Outputs
- **Expedition report:** held by Nigel Gummerson (not published online)
- **Sound recordings:** deposited with BLOWS (British Library of Wildlife Sounds). New Britain Fruit Dove recordings confirmed; New Britain Kingfisher recordings — status unknown, needs checking.
- **CLP project page:** https://www.conservationleadershipprogramme.org/project/nakanai-93-oxford-university-expedition-new-britain-island-papua-new-guinea/

### Legacy
- The expedition is not cited in the major subsequent publications on New Britain birds (Davis *et al.* 2018, Buchanan *et al.* 2008), nor in the RAP Bulletin chapter on Birds of the Nakanai Mountains.
- A 1996 CLP follow-up noted the team leader did not confirm whether any follow-up work occurred.
- The expedition report and any unpublished data may contain valuable baseline information predating the major oil palm expansion period (post-2000).

## Research Gaps

- No formal population census
- Breeding biology entirely unknown
- Diet not studied
- Response to oil palm (whether it uses plantation habitat) not confirmed
- Very few photographs and sound recordings in public databases
- No genetic studies on population structure
